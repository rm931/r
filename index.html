Experience 1.                      Towards sustainable food systems: developing a monitoring framework for the EU

Abstract:

Adequate monitoring of the EU transition toward sustainable food systems can promote policy coherence and provide better evidence for informed policy making. This paper presents an initial concept and methodology for an EU food system monitoring framework, integrating a systems’ perspective and key sustainability elements relevant to the EU context. Grounded in scientific evidence and extensive dialogues among scientific experts with interdisciplinary backgrounds, we define an EU food system sustainability model that provides a conceptual framework for monitoring. This model encompasses 12 thematic areas and 37 indicator domains, synthetized through a rigorous review of existing frameworks and the assessment of nearly 250 relevant indicators via a transparent workflow and an integrated collaborative digital tool. We identify data gaps that signal challenges ahead in effective monitoring, but also opportunities for research and cooperation. To advance with an EU food system monitoring framework, it is essential to engage in participatory processes with stakeholders, ensuring an inclusive and transparent approach.

Research Problem

The paper addresses the lack of a comprehensive and integrated monitoring framework for tracking the EU's transition toward sustainable food systems. Existing monitoring systems in the EU focus on specific aspects of food systems (e.g., agriculture, biodiversity, emissions) but lack a holistic, systems-based approach that captures economic, environmental, and social sustainability.

Methodology

  • Development of the EU Food System Sustainability Model
    • Integrates environmental, economic, and social dimensions of sustainability.
    • Uses a systems perspective grounded in DPSIR (Driver-Pressure-State-Impact-Response) framework to track policy impact.
  • Review of existing global and EU food system models
    • Analyzed ~250 indicators from sources like the FAO, OECD, JRC, and EU agencies.
    • Selected 180 indicators based on quality assessment criteria (relevance, methodological soundness, geographical & temporal coverage).
  • Implementation using the DataM Platform
    • Ensures data interoperability and automation, integrating real-time data from multiple sources.
  • Identification of Data Gaps
    • Identifies lack of comprehensive data in areas like food security, biodiversity, climate change, circular economy, and resilience.

Key Findings

  • Proposed a monitoring framework structured into:
    • 12 thematic areas (e.g., climate change, biodiversity, circular economy, economic viability, food security).
    • 37 indicator domains, covering GHG emissions, food loss, pollution, trade, affordability, and nutrition.
  • Identified significant data gaps in:
    • Climate change impacts by product type.
    • Agricultural pollution and antimicrobial resistance tracking.
    • Circular economy metrics (e.g., reuse of food by-products).
    • Resilience indicators for food systems.
  • Highlighted the need for stakeholder engagement to refine indicators and bridge policy gaps.

Limitations & Future Research

  • Lack of comprehensive data in certain domains (e.g., small-scale fisheries, food affordability, governance participation).
  • Challenges in policy coherence, as food systems involve conflicting sectoral interests.
  • Further integration with EU policy frameworks (e.g., CAP, Farm-to-Fork Strategy, Circular Economy Action Plan) needed.

2. Expertise Demonstration

Relevance to Existing Projects & Methodologies

  • Alignment with FAO & EU Research
    • Uses methodologies from FAO (food system sustainability), JRC (data integration), and the OECD.
  • Integration of Sustainability Metrics
    • Incorporates life cycle thinking (LCA-based food footprints) and DPSIR causal framework.
  • Data Collection & Analysis Techniques
    • Leverages big data tools (DataM platform) to automate indicator tracking.
    • Uses remote sensing, GIS, and statistical modeling for agricultural and environmental indicators.
  • Cross-Disciplinary Approach
    • Combines agronomy, economics, environmental science, and governance studies.

Measurable Outcomes

  • Provides a quantifiable basis for monitoring food system transitions in line with the EU Green Deal & SDGs.
  • Identifies areas where new research and data collection are urgently needed.
  • Sets up a framework to assess the effectiveness of EU food policies in real time.

3. EU Policy Relevance and Impact

Key EU Policies Addressed

  • European Green Deal → Monitoring sustainability progress in food systems.
  • Farm to Fork Strategy (F2F) → Tracking GHG reduction, pollution, circular economy in food systems.
  • Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) → Measuring economic viability and equity in food value chains.
  • Biodiversity Strategy & Zero Pollution Action Plan → Indicators for biodiversity conservation and pollution reduction.
  • EU Soil Strategy → Soil health and land use indicators integrated into monitoring.
  • EU Circular Economy Action Plan → Food waste, material reuse, and consumption footprint tracking.

Impact on EU Policymaking

  • Enhances policy coherence by linking food sustainability goals with measurable indicators.
  • Identifies regulatory gaps in monitoring food system resilience and governance.
  • Supports evidence-based policymaking by integrating multi-sectoral data.

Policy Gaps & Recommendations

  • Governance and participation gaps → Need for structured stakeholder engagement mechanisms.
  • Resilience measurement is underdeveloped → Propose EU-wide resilience index for food systems.
  • Circular economy indicators incomplete → Develop standardized metrics for food waste reduction & bio-based materials use.
  • Need for harmonized data across EU → Implement common reporting standards for food system indicators.

Conclusion

This paper provides a solid foundation for an EU-wide food system monitoring framework, aligning with key sustainability policies and SDGs. While it makes significant progress in defining indicators and methodologies, data gaps and governance challenges remain critical obstacles. The framework's integration with EU policy instruments and real-time monitoring capabilities offer high potential for improving sustainability assessments and policymaking.



Experience 2.                      Delivering the EU Green Deal - Progress towards targets

 

Abstract:

This report provides a comprehensive assessment of progress towards the European Green Deal (EGD), the European Union’s transformative agenda for achieving climate neutrality by 2050. The analysis encompasses 154 quantifiable targets from 44 policy documents between 2019 and 2024 across key sectors such as climate, energy, circular economy, transport, agriculture and food, ecosystems and biodiversity, water, soil and air pollution. The study shows that significant achievement has been delivered so far but progress needs to accelerate in many areas. As of mid-2024, 32 of the 154 targets are currently “on track” and 64 are identified as “acceleration needed” meaning that more progress is needed to meet the targets on time. Furthermore, 15 of the targets are found to be “not progressing” or “regressing”, and for 43 of the targets no data is currently available. The timing of the binding policies, most of which have been recently agreed and are expected to deliver results in the coming years, is a significant factor influencing these assessments. This report integrates all EGD actions and related policies, offering an assessment of the EU’s green transition based on robust data and science. It identifies priority areas for intensified efforts to meet short-term implementation goals and contribute to the long-term ambition of a sustainable, fair, just, and climate-neutral Europe by 2050. This collective work serves as a benchmarking tool, providing scientifically grounded guidance for future EU policies and programmes.

 

1. Summary of the Paper

Research Problem

This report, produced by the Joint Research Centre (JRC) of the European Commission, assesses the progress of the European Green Deal (EGD) by evaluating 154 quantifiable targets from 44 policy documents (2019-2024). The study identifies areas where progress is on track, needs acceleration, or is lacking.

Methodology

  • Data Collection & Policy Screening
    • Analysis of 154 targets from 44 EU policy documents, grouped into 7 thematic areas.
    • Data sourced from Eurostat, the European Environment Agency (EEA), European Parliament reports, and scientific advisory boards.
    • Indicators categorized into:
      • "On Track" (progress sufficient),
      • "Acceleration Needed" (progress insufficient),
      • "Not Progressing" (stagnating or reversing),
      • "No Data Available" (targets too recent or unmeasured).
  • Quantitative & Qualitative Analysis
    • Uses sectoral modeling, climate policy assessments, and impact evaluations.
    • Applies traffic light assessment methodology to categorize targets.
    • Evaluates implementation barriers and synergies across policies.

Key Findings

  • Overall Progress Assessment
    • 32 targets (21%) are on track.
    • 64 targets (41%) need accelerated implementation.
    • 15 targets (10%) show no progress or regression.
    • 43 targets (28%) lack sufficient data.
  • Sector-Specific Challenges
    • Climate Ambition (GHG Reduction)
      • EU is close to meeting its 2030 target of a 55% reduction in net GHG emissions.
      • However, LULUCF (Land Use, Land Use Change, and Forestry) carbon sinks are not progressing.
    • Clean Energy & Circular Economy
      • Expansion of renewables and energy efficiency policies is accelerating, but barriers in grid infrastructure and supply chain dependencies slow progress.
    • Biodiversity & Zero Pollution Targets
      • Biodiversity restoration & pollution reduction measures face implementation delays due to funding gaps and insufficient monitoring frameworks.
    • Agriculture & Farm-to-Fork Strategy
      • Sustainable farming transitions (e.g., organic agriculture) are not meeting expected rates.
      • Pesticide and fertilizer reductions are stagnating, affecting biodiversity & water quality.

Limitations & Future Research

  • Lack of standardized data across Member States hinders assessment.
  • Newly implemented policies (e.g., Fit for 55, Nature Restoration Law) will need years to show measurable impact.
  • Future modeling efforts should integrate socio-economic impacts of the Green Deal.

2. Expertise Demonstration (Projects, Methodologies, Outcomes)

Relevance to EU Sustainability Monitoring

  • Methodologies Used:
    • Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) for circular economy targets.
    • Integrated Climate-Energy-Economic Models for energy transition scenarios.
    • Remote Sensing & GIS Analysis for biodiversity monitoring.
  • Key Data & Tools Applied:
    • EU Reference Scenarios for emissions reduction projections.
    • Traffic light system for policy monitoring, similar to Eurostat’s SDG dashboard.
    • EEA Climate Neutrality Stocktake for emissions tracking.
  • Applications to Ongoing Research:
    • Identifies gaps in EU-wide carbon sequestration and soil restoration policies.
    • Provides a benchmarking tool for EU’s progress towards 2050 climate neutrality.

3. EU Policy Relevance and Impact

Alignment with EU Policy Frameworks

  • European Green Deal & Fit for 55 → Evaluates real-time implementation of climate targets.
  • Biodiversity Strategy & Nature Restoration Law → Assesses progress on habitat restoration & land-use policies.
  • Farm to Fork & CAP → Monitors sustainable agriculture and food system resilience.
  • Circular Economy Action Plan → Tracks resource efficiency, plastic waste reduction & recycling progress.
  • Zero Pollution Action Plan → Measures air, water, and soil pollution mitigation efforts.

Policy Gaps & Recommendations

  • Data Gaps & Standardization Needs
    • Many sustainability indicators lack harmonized EU-wide data collection mechanisms.
    • Member State-level disparities in reporting hinder accurate assessments.
  • Need for Accelerated Implementation
    • Carbon sinks (LULUCF) require urgent interventions to ensure GHG reduction targets are met.
    • Biodiversity goals require more funding & stakeholder engagement.
  • Future EU Policy Integration
    • Introduce legally binding biodiversity & ecosystem resilience targets beyond current voluntary initiatives.
    • Expand green finance incentives to accelerate private-sector alignment with sustainability goals.

Conclusion

This report provides a data-driven evaluation of EU Green Deal progress and highlights critical areas needing acceleration. While the EU is making strides in climate action, clean energy, and circular economy, major implementation challenges persist in biodiversity, pollution reduction, and sustainable agriculture. The findings serve as a benchmark for future EU policy interventions, emphasizing the need for integrated monitoring, enhanced financial support, and increased policy coherence.


Experience 3.                      Policy relevant crop diversity monitoring based on earth observation and farmers' declarations

Abstract:

Comprehensive, wall-to-wall, evaluation of cropping systems and therefore crop diversity using Earth Observation (EO) data is becoming reality. Agricultural policies such as the Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) in the European Union (EU) stimulate more diverse crop-mixes. We compute a new EU-wide overview of crop diversification for 2018 using the Shannon diversity index based on 10 m resolution EO crop map. For the EU as a whole, the effective number of species broadly ranges from 2.6 (p10) to 5.3 (p90) with a median of 3.9. The EO-based map allows identifying how crop diversity varies at an informative spatial resolution, e.g. in areas dominated by mono-cropping or with extensive forest cover. Here we compare the crop diversity calculated from top–down EO-data and bottom–up farmers' declarations in the Netherlands using the EO-based map (more than 84 millions 10 m pixels) and 2018 Dutch farmers declarations (more than 323 thousands parcels). After accounting for differences in thematic and spatial detail we show agreement in spatial patterns and calculated crop diversification (R2 = 0.62). Furthermore, we track changes in crop diversity over time using the Dutch farmers' declarations from 2009 to 2021, which suggest a positive response to the 2014 CAP greening policy. Combining the forthcoming Copernicus High Resolution Layers on Crop Types with increasingly available parcel data will enhance spatial targeting of agricultural policies and facilitate the monitoring of policy impact at farm to regional level.

 

1. Summary of the Paper

Research Problem

This study addresses the need for reliable, EU-wide crop diversity monitoring to evaluate Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) greening measures. Current crop diversity assessments rely on farmers' declarations, which lack harmonization and accessibility across the EU. Earth Observation (EO) data from Copernicus Sentinel satellites offers a potential alternative, but its accuracy compared to farmers’ declarations remains unclear.

Methodology

  • Data Sources:
    • Earth Observation (EO) Data → EU-wide 10m crop maps from Sentinel-1 radar and LUCAS in-situ data.
    • Farmers' Declarations → Dutch parcel-level data from 2009 to 2021 (from CAP subsidy applications).
  • Crop Diversity Metric:
    • Shannon Diversity Index (H'), measuring species richness and evenness.
  • Comparative Analysis:
    • EO vs. Farmers' Declarations in 2018 Netherlands data.
    • Temporal trends (2009-2021) using Dutch farmers' declarations.

Key Findings

  • EU-wide EO-based crop diversity (2018):
    • Median Shannon Diversity Index = 3.9.
    • Higher diversity in Mediterranean regions (e.g., Spain, Italy).
    • Lower diversity in Scandinavia & mono-cropped regions.
  • EO vs. Farmers’ Declarations (Netherlands, 2018):
    • High agreement (R² = 0.62), but EO underestimated diversity in horticultural areas.
  • Temporal Analysis (2009-2021, Netherlands):
    • Crop diversity declined from 2009-2014.
    • Increased from 2015 onwards, correlating with 2014 CAP greening policies.

Limitations & Future Research

  • EO data is less detailed than farmers’ declarations (18 vs. 300+ crop classes).
  • No EU-wide harmonized farmer declaration database exists.
  • Future improvements expected from Copernicus HRL Crop Types dataset (2024+).

2. Expertise Demonstration (Projects, Methodologies, Outcomes)

Methodological Advances

  • Integration of EO and administrative farm data for policy-relevant monitoring.
  • Use of Sentinel-1 Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) data to distinguish crop types.
  • Multi-temporal analysis of crop diversity responses to policy changes.

Applications to Current Research

  • Supports evidence-based CAP evaluations by validating the impact of crop rotation rules.
  • Can be used to refine remote sensing-based agricultural monitoring systems.
  • Lays groundwork for EU-wide, automated biodiversity monitoring tools.

Measurable Outcomes

  • Identifies spatially explicit crop diversity hotspots & gaps.
  • Provides a method for EU Member States to verify CAP compliance.
  • Improves targeting of eco-schemes & agri-environmental incentives.

3. EU Policy Relevance and Impact

Relevant EU Policies

  • Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) (2014-2027) → Evaluates impact of crop rotation & diversification mandates.
  • EU Green Deal & Biodiversity Strategy → Supports agroecological transitions.
  • Copernicus Earth Observation Program → Demonstrates EO’s role in policy implementation.
  • High-Value Dataset Act (2024) → Highlights need for open-access farmer declaration data.

Policy Gaps & Recommendations

  • Need for harmonized crop classification across EU.
  • Expand EO-based monitoring to include functional crop diversity (e.g., nitrogen-fixing crops).
  • Improve accessibility of farmers’ declarations for cross-validation.

Conclusion

This study demonstrates that Earth Observation (EO) data can provide reliable, EU-wide crop diversity assessments but remains limited in thematic detail. Integrating EO with harmonized farmers’ declarations would significantly enhance policy monitoring, particularly for CAP implementation and EU Green Deal objectives.


Experience 4.                      Mapping and Assessment of Ecosystems and their Services: An EU ecosystem assessment

Abstract:

 

 

This report presents an ecosystem assessment covering the total land area of the EU as well as the EU marine regions. The assessment is carried out by Joint Research Centre, European Environment Agency, DG Environment, and the European Topic Centres on Biological Diversity and on Urban, Land and Soil Systems.
This report constitutes a knowledge base which can support the evaluation of the 2020 biodiversity targets. It also provides a data foundation for future assessments and policy developments, in particular with respect to the ecosystem restoration agenda for the next decade (2020-2030).
The report presents an analysis of the pressures and condition of terrestrial, freshwater and marine ecosystems using a single, comparable methodology based on European data on trends of pressures and condition relative to the policy baseline 2010.
The following main conclusions are drawn:
- Pressures on ecosystems exhibit different trends.
- Land take, atmospheric emissions of air pollutants and critical loads of nitrogen are decreasing but the absolute values of all these pressures remain too high.
- Impacts from climate change on ecosystems are increasing.
- Invasive alien species of union concern are observed in all ecosystems, but their impact is particularly high in urban ecosystems and grasslands.
- Pressures from overfishing activities and marine pollution are still high.
- In the long term, air and freshwater quality is improving.
- In forests and agroecosystems, which represent over 80% of the EU territory, there are improvements in structural condition indicators (biomass, deadwood, area under organic farming) relative to the baseline year 2010 but some key bio-indicators such as tree-crown defoliation continue to increase. This indicates that ecosystem condition is not improving.
- Species-related indicators show no progress or further declines, particularly in agroecosystems.
The analysis of trends in ecosystem services concluded that the current potential of ecosystems to deliver timber, protection against floods, crop pollination, and nature-based recreation is equal to or lower than the baseline value for 2010. At the same time, the demand for these services has significantly increased. A lowered potential in combination with a higher demand risks to further decrease the condition of ecosystems and their contribution to human well-being.
Despite the wide coverage of environmental legislation in the EU, there are still large gaps in the legal protection of ecosystems. On land, 76% of the area of terrestrial ecosystems, mainly forests, agroecosystems and urban ecosystems, are excluded from a legal designation under the Bird and Habitat Directives.
Freshwater and marine ecosystems are subject to specific protection measures under the Water Framework and Marine Strategy Framework Directives. The condition of ecosystems that are under legal designation is unfavourable.
More efforts are needed to bend the curve of biodiversity loss and ecosystem degradation and to put ecosystems on a path to recovery.
The progress that is made in certain areas such as pollution reduction, increasing air and water quality, increasing share of organic farming, the expansion of forests, and the efforts to maintain marine fish stocks at sustainable levels show that a persistent implementation of policies can be effective. These successes should encourage us to act now and to put forward an ambitious plan for the restoration of Europe’s ecosystems.

1. Summary of the Paper

Research Problem

This report provides the first EU-wide ecosystem assessment, aiming to:

  1. Evaluate trends in ecosystem extent, condition, and services based on the 2010 baseline.
  2. Support the EU Biodiversity Strategy to 2020 & 2030.
  3. Identify policy gaps in ecosystem conservation and restoration.

Methodology

  • Data Sources:
    • Copernicus CORINE Land Cover Data (2018)
    • EU Birds and Habitats Directives conservation reports
    • Water Framework & Marine Strategy Framework Directives
    • Soil, air, and biodiversity monitoring datasets
  • Assessment Framework:
    • Uses Mapping and Assessment of Ecosystems and Services (MAES) methodology.
    • Analyzes 7 terrestrial ecosystems (forests, agroecosystems, urban, wetlands, etc.), freshwater, and marine ecosystems.
    • Applies pressure-impact-response analysis to evaluate ecosystem health.

Key Findings

  • Ecosystem Extent & Change
    • Forests & croplands dominate (36% each) of EU land.
    • Urban areas expanded by 3.4% per decade, mainly at the cost of agricultural land.
    • Wetlands & grasslands declined, increasing vulnerability to climate change.
  • Ecosystem Condition
    • 76% of terrestrial ecosystems lack legal protection under the Birds & Habitats Directives.
    • Only 14% of protected forests & 10.7% of wetlands are in favorable conservation status.
    • Air & freshwater quality improving, but marine ecosystems show continued degradation.
  • Ecosystem Services
    • Timber provision & carbon sequestration stable.
    • Crop pollination & flood protection services declining.
    • Rising demand for ecosystem services exceeds supply, worsening environmental stress.

Limitations & Future Research

  • Insufficient monitoring of marine ecosystems (e.g., Mediterranean & Black Sea).
  • Lack of data on soil degradation trends in croplands.
  • Need for better harmonization of biodiversity monitoring across EU Member States.

Measurable Outcomes

The findings directly inform EU’s Green Deal & Biodiversity Strategy, supporting a more data-driven approach to ecosystem management.


Experience 5.                      Accounting for ecosystems and their services in the European Union (INCA)

Abstract:

 

The EU INCA project was launched in 2015 to produce a pilot for an integrated system of ecosystem accounting for the EU, while serving as a large-scale test case for the first UN handbook on ecosystem accounting System of Environmental-Economic Accounting – Experimental Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EEA) published in 2014. The results and findings of the INCA project provided feedback for the revised version of the UN handbook SEEA – Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA), completed and adopted in March 2021. The INCA project has shown that the production of a wide range of ecosystem accounts following the guidance of the SEEA EEA is feasible and it is possible to produce consistent and comparable information on ecosystems and the services they provide to society at the scale of the EU. This report summarises key results of the INCA project achieved by 2020. It presents ecosystem extent accounts (for 9 broad types of ecosystems), ecosystem condition accounts (for forests, agro-ecosystems and rivers and lakes) and ecosystem services accounts (for a subset of ecosystem services) for the EU. In separate chapters, the report also presents practical examples of the possible uses of ecosystem services accounts and existing policy applications. In addition, the report outlines crucial steps for making ecosystem accounts operational. Ecosystems contribute essential services to the economy and society. These include the provision of food, filtration of air and water, climate regulation, protection against extreme weather events such as heat waves and flooding, and many more. The ability of ecosystems to supply these services depends on their extent (‘size’) and condition (‘health’). Despite the crucial role of ecosystems and their services for society, there is no established and regular measurement of ecosystem extent, condition and their change over time, nor of the quantity of services these ecosystems supply. Ecosystem accounting is an emerging field that aims to address this major gap and provide an internationally agreed guidance to measure and record changes in ecosystems and ecosystem services in a consistent and comparable manner. Many of the services supplied by ecosystems are public goods and are not currently priced on markets and, consequently, are often not taken into account in economic decisions. This has had disastrous consequences for the natural world, and in turn, for society. Ecosystem accounting has adopted the language and guiding principles of economic accounts (System of National Accounts) that will enable ecosystems and their services to be properly incorporated into standard accounting frameworks, and thus allow for the value of nature to be included more fully in decision making.

 

1. Summary of the Paper

Research Problem

The INCA (Integrated Natural Capital Accounting) project was launched in 2015 to develop a pilot ecosystem accounting system for the EU. The report presents a comprehensive assessment of ecosystem extent, condition, and services, aligned with the UN System of Environmental-Economic Accounting – Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA) framework. The key goals are:

  • Track ecosystem changes over time (2000–2018).
  • Quantify ecosystem service contributions (e.g., pollination, carbon sequestration, water purification).
  • Bridge ecological and economic decision-making.

Methodology

  • Ecosystem Accounts Framework:
    • Extent Accounts → Track area changes in different ecosystem types.
    • Condition Accounts → Assess ecosystem health based on physical, chemical, and biological indicators.
    • Ecosystem Service Accounts → Quantify ecosystem benefits in both physical and monetary terms.
    • Economic Valuation → Assign monetary value to ecosystem services.
  • Data Sources:
    • Copernicus CORINE Land Cover Data (2000–2018).
    • Water Framework & Marine Strategy Framework Directives.
    • EEA and Eurostat biodiversity, soil, air, and water datasets.
    • CAPRI agro-economic model for nutrient input estimates.

Key Findings

  • Ecosystem Extent Trends (2000–2018):
    • Urban areas expanded by 5.8%, mainly at the cost of agricultural land.
    • Croplands and grasslands declined (-0.5% and -0.8%, respectively).
    • Wetlands and heathlands experienced further loss (-0.5% and -1.2%).
    • Forests remained stable overall, but forest composition changed (increase in mixed forests, decline in coniferous forests).
  • Ecosystem Condition:
    • 76% of terrestrial ecosystems remain unprotected.
    • Forests showed increasing biomass but declining tree health (higher defoliation rates).
    • Water quality improved, but river and lake ecological status remains poor (46% of rivers in good status).
    • Grassland biodiversity declining (farmland bird and butterfly index both down by ~30% since 1990).
  • Ecosystem Service Valuation (2012 Data):
    • Total value of ecosystem services in the EU: €172 billion annually.
    • Water purification (€55.6B) and nature-based recreation (€50.4B) are the highest-valued services.
    • Forests provide nearly half (47.5%) of all measured ecosystem services.
    • Agriculture is the largest consumer of ecosystem services (38% of total use).

Limitations & Future Research

  • Lack of harmonized data on ecosystem condition across Member States.
  • Underestimation of marine and soil ecosystem services.
  • Need for improved monitoring of pollination deficits and invasive species impacts.

Relevance to EU Natural Capital Accounting & Monitoring

  • Advances the MAES Framework → Provides an EU-wide baseline for ecosystem condition tracking.
  • Integration of Remote Sensing & Big Data → Uses Copernicus EO & GIS for spatial ecosystem mapping.
  • Economic Valuation Methods → Applies SEEA EA framework to monetize ecosystem services.

Applications to Ongoing Research

3. EU Policy Relevance and Impact

Key EU Policies Addressed

  • EU Biodiversity Strategy 2030 → Supports habitat restoration & nature-based solutions.
  • European Green Deal → Provides data for carbon sequestration & climate adaptation tracking.
  • Common Agricultural Policy (CAP) & Eco-Schemes → Helps assess land-use sustainability.
  • EU Nature Restoration Law → Identifies priority sites for large-scale restoration efforts.
  • EU Water & Marine Framework Directives → Assesses freshwater & marine ecosystem health.

Policy Gaps & Recommendations

  • Expand legal protection → Strengthen protected area networks beyond Natura 2000.
  • Prioritize wetland & grassland restoration → Improve carbon sequestration & flood resilience.
  • Improve cross-sectoral monitoring → Enhance integration of biodiversity data into climate & agricultural policies.
  • Develop standardized ecosystem service valuation methods → Support business & public sector decision-making.

Conclusion

The INCA project represents a milestone in EU ecosystem accounting, offering critical insights for biodiversity conservation, climate resilience, and sustainable land-use policymaking. The findings directly inform the EU Green Deal & Biodiversity Strategy, supporting a data-driven approach to ecosystem management.
