From Parcels to People: Development of a Spatially Explicit Risk Indicator to Monitor Residential Pesticide Exposure in Agricultural Areas

1. Abstract

Pesticide use in agriculture is a growing concern due to its effects on biodiversity, ecosystems, and human health, particularly for people living near agricultural areas. This study introduces a spatially explicit risk indicator to monitor residential exposure to pesticides, leveraging high-resolution pesticide application data from France. The method integrates:

  • Parcel-level pesticide load mapping using plant protection product (PPP) sales data,
  • Population distribution modeling, and
  • Exposure risk calculations based on EFSA guidance.

Findings suggest that 13% of the French population resides near pesticide-treated crops, with 26% experiencing moderate to high exposure levels. The risk indicator enables targeted policy interventions and sustainable land management strategies across the EU. It is scalable for EU-wide monitoring, supporting the European Green Deal and Zero Pollution Action Plan.

2. Summary

Research Problem

  • EU policies such as the Farm to Fork Strategy aim to reduce pesticide use by 50% by 2030, yet current monitoring tools lack spatial granularity.
  • Existing Harmonized Risk Indicators (HRIs) rely on national pesticide sales data, which are insufficient for local risk assessments.
  • There is no EU-wide indicator to assess non-dietary exposure risks for populations living near agricultural areas.

Methodology

  1. Data Sources
    • BNV-d Pesticide Sales Database (France, 2013-2021): High-resolution sales data by active substance (AS).
    • Registre Parcellaire Graphique (RPG): Crop parcel mapping.
    • EUROSTAT & National Census Data: Residential population distribution.
    • LUCAS Soil Database: Pesticide residues in soil for model validation.
  2. Spatial Risk Modeling
    • Step 1: Pesticide Load Mapping
      • 388 active substances mapped at the parcel level.
      • Example: Fluroxypyr herbicide application near Orléans.
    • Step 2: Exposure Risk Estimation
      • Calculated pesticide exposure risks via four exposure pathways:
        1. Vapour inhalation,
        2. Spray drift,
        3. Entry into treated fields,
        4. Dermal transfer.
      • Step 3: Risk Indicator Development
        • Pesticide load data combined with population distribution maps.
        • Results classified into five exposure risk levels at postcode level.

Key Findings

  1. Residential Pesticide Exposure
    • 13% of the French population lives near treated agricultural parcels.
    • 74% experience low exposure, while 26% face moderate to high exposure risks.
    • Crops with the highest exposure risks: vineyards, sugar beets, spring barley, potatoes.
  2. Regional Risk Patterns
    • High-risk areas: Bordeaux, Champagne, Burgundy, Loire Valley (wine regions), Brittany, Amiens, Orléans (intensive agriculture).
    • Lower risk: Mediterranean coastal areas, natural parks, urban centers (Paris, Lyon, Rennes, Nantes, Toulouse).
  3. Model Validation
    • Pesticide residue data from LUCAS Soil Database confirmed spatial exposure patterns.
    • Correlation with observed soil pesticide residues was moderate due to data limitations.
  4. Potential Applications
    • Supports EU-wide risk monitoring of non-dietary pesticide exposure.
    • Helps identify high-risk zones for sustainable agricultural transitions.

Relevance to Environmental and Climate Science

  • Integration of multiple datasets (pesticide sales, crop mapping, census data).

Innovative Methodologies

    • Uses EFSA’s Acceptable Operator Exposure Level (AOEL) as a reference.
    • Risk quotient calculation for pesticide exposure pathways.
    • Geostatistical classification into five exposure categories.
  • Comparison with existing pesticide risk models:
    • Treatment Frequency Index (TFI) vs. Aggregated Distribution of Active Substances.
    • Identifies gaps in current pesticide risk evaluation methods.

Real-World Applications

    • Supports CAP (Common Agricultural Policy) eco-schemes for pesticide reduction.

  • Agricultural sustainability:

    • Promotes eco-friendly pest management alternatives.

Accounting for Ecosystems and their Services in the European Union (INCA)

The report focuses on ecosystem accounting in the EU, assessing ecosystem extent, condition, and services using an integrated approach.

Abstract

The INCA (Integrated system of Natural Capital and ecosystem services Accounting) project, initiated in 2015, aims to develop a pilot for an integrated system of ecosystem accounts for the EU.

The INCA project aims to establish a pilot system for ecosystem accounting in the EU, integrating ecological and economic data to quantify natural capital. The study applies the System of Environmental-Economic Accounting – Ecosystem Accounting (SEEA EA) framework to develop accounts for ecosystem extent, condition, and services. It follows the SEEA (System of Environmental-Economic Accounting) framework, producing ecosystem extent, condition, and services accounts. The report summarizes key results of the project, demonstrating the feasibility of ecosystem accounting at the EU scale. Findings indicate changes in ecosystem extent, variations in ecosystem condition, and the economic value of ecosystem services, contributing significantly to EU policy-making and sustainability goals.

Key findings include:

  • Ecosystem extent trends (2000–2018): Urban areas expanded by 5.8%, while cropland, grasslands, and inland wetlands declined.
  • Ecosystem condition: Forest health indicators showed mixed trends, with nitrogen pollution decreasing but defoliation increasing. Agro-ecosystems saw a moderate rise in organic farming (+55%) but continued biodiversity decline.
  • Ecosystem services valuation: The total annual value of seven key ecosystem services (e.g., carbon sequestration, water purification, pollination) in the EU was estimated at €172 billion in 2012.
    The report highlights ecosystem accounting as a crucial tool for evidence-based policymaking, supporting initiatives like the EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030 and the European Green Deal.

Summary

Research Problem

The report addresses the lack of systematic, standardized ecosystem accounting in the EU. Despite ecosystems providing critical services—such as carbon sequestration, pollination, and water purification—there is no unified method to integrate them into economic decision-making. The INCA project seeks to bridge this gap by applying ecosystem accounting based on the SEEA EA framework.

Methodologies

  • Ecosystem Extent Accounts: Developed using Copernicus Corine Land Cover (CLC) data, classifying ecosystems into three tiers of increasing detail.
  • Ecosystem Condition Accounts: Tracked forest, agro-ecosystem, and freshwater health using indicators like nitrogen pollution, soil organic matter, and biodiversity indices.
  • Ecosystem Services Accounts: Evaluated the supply and value of seven ecosystem services, including crop pollination, timber provision, and carbon sequestration, using physical and monetary units.
  • Data Integration: Combined geospatial, statistical, and economic datasets from Eurostat, JRC, EEA, and Copernicus.

Key Findings

1. Ecosystem Extent (2000–2018)
  • Urban areas increased by 5.8%, primarily in coastal regions, leading to a loss of cropland (-0.5%) and grassland (-0.8%).
  • Forests remained stable but saw internal shifts, with mixed forests increasing (+3%) at the expense of coniferous forests (-4.6%).
  • Natura 2000 protected areas had greater ecosystem stability, showing smaller losses than unprotected areas.
2. Ecosystem Condition Trends
  • Forests:
    • Eutrophication and ozone damage decreased (~-30%), but defoliation worsened (+10%).
    • Biomass and deadwood stock increased (~+10%), indicating better carbon storage.
  • Agro-ecosystems:
    • Organic farming expanded (+55%), but overall cropland area shrank.
    • Farmland biodiversity declined, with farmland bird and butterfly indices falling by 1–1.3% per decade.
  • Rivers and Lakes:
    • Water quality improved (e.g., nitrogen and phosphorus pollution declined).
    • Floodplain urbanization increased (+7%), heightening flood risks.
3. Ecosystem Services Valuation (2012, EU28)
  • Total value of seven key ecosystem services: €172 billion/year, comparable to the GVA of agriculture and forestry combined.
  • Top services:
    • Water purification (€55.6B)
    • Recreation in natural areas (€50.4B)
    • Crop provision (€20.8B)
  • Forests supply 47.5% of all ecosystem services, while cropland contributes 36%.
  • More than half of societal demand for ecosystem services is unmet, highlighting the need for ecosystem restoration.

Expertise Demonstration (Projects, Methodologies, Outcomes)

The INCA project aligns with EU research initiatives and decision-making frameworks on natural capital accounting, ecosystem monitoring, and sustainability policy evaluation. Key connections include:

  • EU-wide application of SEEA EA methodology, supporting Eurostat, JRC, and EEA in advancing environmental-economic accounts.
  • Use of Copernicus satellite data for ecosystem classification, mirroring approaches in projects like MAES (Mapping and Assessment of Ecosystems and their Services) and LUCAS Land Use Surveys.
  • Integration with biodiversity conservation frameworks such as the EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030 and CAP (Common Agricultural Policy) Eco-Schemes.

Key Methodological Contributions:

  • Spatial nutrient accounts: Assessed nitrogen inputs to agro-ecosystems, linking land-use practices to water quality.
  • Pollination accounts: Modeled the economic effects of pollinator decline, supporting the EU Pollinators Initiative.
  • Ecosystem service trade analysis: Quantified 'exported' ecosystem services (e.g., pollination, water purification) embedded in agricultural trade flows.

EU Policy Relevance and Impact

Alignment with EU Sustainability Goals

  • European Green Deal & Biodiversity Strategy 2030: Supports goals for ecosystem restoration, carbon neutrality, and sustainable land use.
  • EU Pollinators Initiative: Provides economic valuation of pollination services to inform pollinator conservation policies.
  • Nitrates Directive & CAP reform: Highlights the need for nutrient reduction measures and support for sustainable farming practices.
  • EU Nature Restoration Plan: Provides essential data for guiding large-scale ecosystem restoration projects.

Policy Development and Implementation

  • Natural Capital Accounting in National Policies: Demonstrates how EU Member States can integrate ecosystem accounts into economic planning.
  • Application in EU Environmental Assessments: INCA data was used in the first EU-wide Ecosystem Assessment (2020), influencing conservation policies.
  • Supports evidence-based policymaking by providing quantitative data on ecosystem health and services.
  • Enhances the monitoring of Natura 2000 areas, ensuring their effectiveness in protecting biodiversity.

Regulatory & Decision-Making Implications

  • Supports the EU Restoration Law by identifying priority areas for habitat recovery.
  • Guides CAP Strategic Plans by quantifying environmental benefits of agri-environment measures.
  • Facilitates EU Taxonomy for Sustainable Finance by providing monetary estimates of ecosystem services to support green investment frameworks.

Final Remarks

The INCA project marks a significant step toward mainstreaming ecosystem accounting in EU policymaking. By quantifying natural capital, it strengthens evidence-based environmental policies, reinforcing the European Green Deal, the Biodiversity Strategy for 2030, and the CAP reform. The use of SEEA EA frameworks ensures international comparability, making this approach replicable for global sustainability assessments.

Landscape Features Support Natural Pest Control and Farm Income When Pesticide

1. Abstract

This study evaluates the role of landscape features supporting natural pest control (LF-NPC) in maintaining agricultural productivity under reduced pesticide use. Using the European Union as a case study, the research integrates granular farm-level data, a high-resolution LF-NPC potential map, and a regional agro-economic supply model. The findings reveal that farms in high LF-NPC potential areas experience lower productivity losses under pesticide reduction policies. The results indicate that LF-NPC reduces yield gaps by approximately 4 percentage points and enhances farm income by a similar margin. These insights provide a strong foundation for policies promoting landscape-based solutions to mitigate the effects of pesticide reduction targets outlined in the European Green Deal and the Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework.

2. Summary

Research Problem

With global and EU policies targeting a 50% reduction in pesticide use by 2030, there is concern over potential productivity losses in agriculture. This study investigates whether landscape features that enhance natural pest control can compensate for pesticide reductions and support farm incomes.

Methodology

  • Data Sources:
    • Yield observations from the EU Farm Accountancy Data Network (FADN)
    • A high-resolution (100m) LF-NPC potential map based on landscape complexity and field observations
    • A European agro-economic model (CAPRI - Common Agricultural Policy Regionalized Impact Modeling System)
  • Analysis:
    • Yield gap estimation: Differences between organic and conventional farming yields were calculated using regression models, isolating the impact of pesticide use.

    • Economic modeling: CAPRI was used to project agricultural economic outcomes under a pesticide reduction scenario.

  2. Landscape features mitigate productivity loss:

  3. Economic benefits:
    • A one-unit increase in LF-NPC potential led to a 7% increase in farm revenue and a 4.2% increase in farm income per hectare.
    • Financial benefits varied across regions due to differences in agricultural structure and crop types.
  4. Policy relevance:

Bee Pollination Outperforms Pesticides for Oilseed Crop Production and Profitability

1. Abstract

The study evaluates the relative contributions of bee pollination and pesticide use to oilseed rape (OSR) yield and farm profitability. While OSR can self-pollinate, bee pollination significantly enhances seed yield. However, pesticide use negatively affects pollinators, potentially reducing these benefits.

Using a large-scale dataset of 294 OSR fields (2013-2016) in France, the study quantifies the individual and combined effects of pesticide application, soil quality, and pollinator abundance on crop yield and farm economics. Findings indicate that increasing pollinator abundance leads to 15-40% higher yield and gross margins, while pesticides do not significantly increase yields but reduce profitability due to high costs. The study highlights the economic and ecological benefits of nature-based solutions (NBS) in agriculture.

2. Summary

Research Problem

  • Trade-off between pollination services and pesticide use: Pesticides may control pests but can harm pollinators, reducing their positive effects on yield.

    • Linear mixed models (LMMs) were used to evaluate yield and profitability in relation to pollination and pesticide use.
    • Interaction effects between bees, soil quality, and agrochemical inputs were analyzed.

A Screening Study of the Spatial Distribution and Cumulative Toxicity of Agricultural Pesticides in the European Union’s Waters

1. Abstract

This study evaluates the distribution and cumulative toxicity of 148 pesticide active substances (AS) across the European Union’s water bodies. Due to data scarcity and limited monitoring, the researchers employ a screening-level model to estimate pesticide concentrations in soil and water. The model's predictions are validated against monitoring data, showing reasonable alignment. Findings indicate that while individual pesticide toxicity remains below 0.1 toxic units (TU) in most areas, cumulative toxicity exceeds 0.1 TU in 27% of the EU’s stream network and 1 TU in 4%. A small number of active substances dominate the cumulative toxicity burden, though variations exist between locations. The study highlights the need for improved pesticide monitoring and more comprehensive emission data to refine EU pesticide policy assessments.

2. Summary

Research Problem

Agricultural pesticides pose a significant threat to aquatic ecosystems, but their spatial distribution and cumulative toxicity remain inadequately characterized due to data limitations and inconsistent monitoring efforts across the EU.

Methodology

Synthesising Results of Meta-Analyses to Inform Policy: A Comparison of Fast-Track Methods

1. Abstract

This study assesses different methods for synthesizing meta-analyses (MAs) to provide scientific evidence for environmental and agricultural policy decision-making. With the exponential increase in MAs, conflicting conclusions often arise, necessitating efficient synthesis methods. The paper evaluates three fast-track methods—Second-Order Meta-Analysis (SOMA), Most Accurate Meta-Analysis (MAMA), and Vote Counting of Meta-Analysis Results (COMA)—and compares them against a global meta-analysis of primary data (REMA). Using simulated data, the study finds that SOMA and COMA yield conclusions similar to REMA when redundancy between first-order MAs is low. However, biases in first-order MAs can significantly impact reliability. The study highlights the trade-offs between speed and accuracy in evidence synthesis, with policy relevance implications.

2. Summary

Research Problem

Environmental and agricultural policymaking increasingly relies on meta-analyses, yet multiple MAs on a given topic can present conflicting conclusions. Decision-makers often require rapid evidence synthesis, but traditional methods are time-consuming. This study evaluates fast-track synthesis approaches and their reliability.

Methodology

  • Methods Evaluated:
    • Second-Order Meta-Analysis (SOMA): Computes a weighted average of first-order MAs' mean effect sizes.
    • Most Accurate Meta-Analysis (MAMA): Selects the MA with the lowest coefficient of variation.
    • Vote Counting of MA Results (COMA): Classifies MAs into positive, negative, or no-effect categories based on significance.
    • Reference Meta-Analysis (REMA): Aggregates all primary data from first-order MAs, serving as the benchmark.
  • Simulated Data:
    • Generated to evaluate accuracy under different conditions (e.g., effect size, redundancy between MAs, publication bias).
    • 720 scenarios tested, covering varying numbers of first-order MAs, sample sizes, redundancy levels, and bias conditions.
  • Evaluation Metrics:
    • Probability of Correct Conclusion (PCC): Accuracy in determining effect direction.
    • Bias and Root Mean Square Error (RMSE): Accuracy of effect size estimation.
    • Confidence Interval Coverage (CCI): Reliability of effect size confidence intervals.

Key Findings

  • Performance of Methods:
    • REMA consistently delivers the most accurate results but is time-intensive.
    • SOMA provides reasonable estimates but loses accuracy when MAs share redundant primary data.
    • MAMA is prone to bias, often selecting extreme effect sizes.
    • COMA lacks statistical power but is reliable in detecting the absence of effects.
  • Impact of Publication Bias:
    • Selective publication of studies with significant effects skews results.
    • Bias favoring positive effects leads to incorrect conclusions in all methods.
    • Bias impact is most severe in MAMA, which tends to amplify extreme findings.

Conclusions

  • SOMA and COMA can serve as practical alternatives for fast-track synthesis when primary data are unavailable.
  • MAMA is unreliable, particularly in the presence of bias.
  • Policy decisions should prioritize REMA, but when time-constrained, SOMA with careful quality checks is the best alternative.
